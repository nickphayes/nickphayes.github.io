Frontier AI is constrained by several technical factors, chief among them hardware, software, and data[^a]. When it comes to hardware, CPUs were replaced by GPUs, which, though arguably still dominant, now co-exist alongside dedicated tensor processing units (TPUs)[(TPUs)](insert url to link explaining TPUs). Within software, rule-based approaches and shallow algorithms gave way to vanilla neural networks, which were soon replaced by more sophisticated models (e.g. CNNs, RNNs, LSTMs), leading to the current era of attention-based transformers. 


Historically, paradigm transitions have occurred following breakthroughs in one or more of these areas. The CPU gave way to the GPU, which allowed deep learning methods to replace shallow algorithms and rule-based approaches. Vanilla neural networks were replaced by models more capable of handling spatial/sequential dependencies (CNNs, RNNs, LSTMs), which, though first developed in the 20th century, only regained popularity with concurrent improvements in datasets (e.g. CIFAR) and compute infrastructure. These models were soon engulfed by transformer-based approaches, while datasets have continually increased in both size and quality. 

Today, the most popular paradigm for pushing the frontier of AI relies on the **scaling hypothesis**, which suggests that we can achieve significant improvements in AI simply by scaling up the size of our models, data, and compute infrastructure.
